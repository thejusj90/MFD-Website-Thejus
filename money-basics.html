Money Basics: Everything You Should Know Before You Invest

The handful of ideas everything else builds on — in the order that actually matters, not the order most people learn them in.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated Jan 10, 2026 7 min read

Quick answer: Before you invest anywhere, get these four things in place, in this order — essential expenses covered, a starter emergency fund of 1 month's expenses, high-interest debt cleared, then a full 3–6 month emergency fund. Only after that does investing meaningfully compound in your favour instead of being undone by a single bad month.

Most personal finance content starts with "which fund should I buy" or "how much return can I expect." Those are fair questions, but they're the wrong ones to start with. The order you do things in matters more than which specific product you pick — get the order wrong, and even a great investment decision can be undone by a bad month.

Key takeaways

  • Order of operations beats product selection — sequence what you do before you decide what to buy.
  • An emergency fund isn't optional or "nice to have" — it's what prevents you from selling investments at a loss.
  • High-interest debt (12%+) should almost always be cleared before you start investing meaningfully.
  • The 50-30-20 split is a starting benchmark, not a rule — adjust it to your actual fixed costs.

The order of operations, step by step

Here's the sequence, and why each step comes before the next:

  • 1. Cover essential expenses. Rent, food, utilities, transport, insurance premiums. This sounds obvious, but the point is to know the exact number — most people underestimate it by 15–20%.
  • 2. Build a starter emergency fund (1 month of expenses). This is your first buffer against a surprise expense before you've built the full fund.
  • 3. Clear high-interest debt. Credit card balances and personal loans usually cost 12–40% annually. No mutual fund reliably beats that after tax, so this debt should go before investing.
  • 4. Build a full emergency fund (3–6 months of expenses). See the Emergency Fund Calculator to size this for your situation.
  • 5. Start investing toward specific goals. Retirement, a house down payment, a child's education — investing works best when it's attached to a named goal and time horizon, not just "growing money" in the abstract.
The order you do things in matters more than which specific fund you pick.

Why the order matters more than people think

Imagine two people who each start a ₹10,000/month SIP on the same day. Person A has a 6-month emergency fund already. Person B doesn't. Six months in, both face an unexpected ₹80,000 medical bill.

Person A pays from their emergency fund and the SIP continues uninterrupted. Person B has to redeem their fund units — possibly at a loss if the market dipped that month — losing both the money and months of compounding. The SIP itself wasn't the problem; the missing foundation was.

💡
A common mistake is treating the emergency fund as something to build "eventually, once investing is going well." In practice, it should come first — it's what protects the investing, not something that competes with it.

A simple starting split: 50-30-20

If you're not sure how to divide your take-home income, the 50-30-20 rule is a reasonable starting benchmark:

  • 50% — Needs: rent, groceries, utilities, insurance, minimum debt payments
  • 30% — Wants: eating out, travel, subscriptions, hobbies
  • 20% — Savings & investing: emergency fund, SIPs, retirement contributions

This isn't a rigid law — someone with high rent in a metro city, or paying off a home loan, may need to adjust the split until fixed costs come down or income grows. The point isn't the exact percentages; it's having a deliberate split instead of spending first and seeing what's left.

What to do next

If you don't yet have a sized emergency fund target, that's the single highest-leverage next step — use the Emergency Fund Calculator to get a number for your situation.
