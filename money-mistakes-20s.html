Common Money Mistakes to Avoid in Your 20s

The patterns that quietly cost people the most in their 20s — and how to sidestep them before they compound.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated Jan 18, 2026 7 min read

Quick answer: The costliest patterns in your 20s are usually: delaying investing "until things settle," lifestyle inflation matching every raise, ignoring a written budget, carrying high-interest debt while trying to invest, and treating insurance as optional. None of these are about lacking discipline — they're about not having a system in place yet.

Your 20s are the highest-leverage decade for compounding, simply because time is the input that matters most. The mistakes below aren't about being reckless — they're quiet, easy-to-miss patterns that cost the most precisely because nobody notices them happening.

Key takeaways

  • Waiting for the "right time" to start investing is the single costliest delay — time matters more than amount.
  • Lifestyle inflation (spending rising with every raise) quietly erases the benefit of earning more.
  • Carrying high-interest debt while investing elsewhere usually loses money on net.
  • Skipping health insurance in your 20s because you feel healthy is a bet, not a plan.

Mistake 1: Waiting for the "right time" to start

"I'll start investing once I get a raise / clear this debt / feel more settled" is one of the most common things people tell themselves — and it usually pushes the actual start date back by years. Because of compounding, the years in your 20s are worth disproportionately more than the same amount invested a decade later. Starting with ₹1,000/month today beats waiting two years to start with ₹5,000/month.

Mistake 2: Letting lifestyle expand with every raise

It's natural for spending to rise a little with income — the mistake is letting it rise by the same amount as the raise, so your savings rate never actually improves no matter how much you earn. A simple guardrail: whenever income rises, commit at least half the increase to savings/investing before it becomes part of your regular spending.

The years in your 20s are worth more than the same money invested a decade later.

Mistake 3: No written budget or spending visibility

Most people don't overspend on one big thing — they overspend gradually across many small, unrecorded purchases. A basic monthly tracking habit (even a simple spreadsheet) usually surfaces 10–15% of "invisible" spending that's easy to redirect once it's visible.

Mistake 4: Investing while carrying high-interest debt

If you're paying 18-40% annual interest on a credit card while investing in a fund expected to return 12%, you're losing money on net every month, even though it feels like you're "doing both." As covered in Money Basics, high-interest debt should almost always be cleared before investing meaningfully elsewhere.

💡
Health insurance is easy to deprioritize in your 20s because you feel healthy — but a single hospitalization without cover can undo years of careful saving. A basic individual health policy, taken early, also tends to be cheaper and comes with fewer waiting-period exclusions than one taken later.

Mistake 5: Treating insurance as optional

Beyond health cover, a simple term life policy is worth considering early if anyone depends on your income, even partially. Term insurance is at its cheapest in your 20s, purely because premiums are priced on age and health.

What to do next

If several of these feel familiar, the fix usually isn't a dramatic overhaul — it's picking one at a time. Start with whichever feels most relevant to you, or bring the full picture to a free 30-minute call and work through it together.
