Why Most People Fail at Building Wealth (It's Not What You Think)

It's rarely a knowledge gap that derails a financial plan — it's what people do under pressure, in the moments no calculator accounts for.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated May 22, 2024 6 min read

Quick answer: Most wealth-building failures aren't caused by picking the wrong investment — they're caused by behaviour: stopping SIPs during a downturn, letting lifestyle rise with every raise, and abandoning a plan the first time it feels uncomfortable. Knowledge rarely fails; discipline under pressure does.

Ask most people why their investing plan didn't work out, and they'll usually blame the fund, the market, or bad timing. In practice, the plans that fail rarely fail because of the product chosen — they fail because of what the investor did in the middle of the journey, usually under some form of stress.

Key takeaways

  • Most failed financial plans fail on behaviour, not on fund selection.
  • Stopping a SIP during a downturn is one of the single most value-destroying habits investors have.
  • Income level matters less than savings rate — high earners without a savings rate build less wealth than assumed.
  • A plan that's "good enough" and followed consistently beats a "perfect" plan abandoned halfway.

It's rarely a knowledge problem

Most people who've read even a little about investing already know the basics: start early, diversify, stay consistent. The gap isn't in what people know — it's in what they actually do when their portfolio is down 15% and every instinct says to stop or exit.

The plans that fail rarely fail because of the product chosen.

The three behaviours that quietly derail most plans

  • Stopping SIPs during a downturn — exactly when unit prices are lower and continuing would benefit them most.
  • Letting lifestyle rise with every raise — so the savings rate stays flat even as income grows.
  • Switching funds chasing last year's topper — usually locking in a loss on the way out and buying in high on the way in.
💡
None of this is about willpower in the abstract sense — it's about designing a system where the "hard" behaviour (staying invested, not checking the portfolio daily) requires the least effort, and the "easy" behaviour (panicking, switching) requires more friction.

What to do instead

Automate what you can — a SIP with the mandate already set removes the daily decision to invest. And build a written plan before a downturn happens, not during one, so decisions are made calmly rather than reactively.
