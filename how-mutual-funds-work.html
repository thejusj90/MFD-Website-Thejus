How Mutual Funds Actually Work

What you're actually buying when you invest in a mutual fund, and why 'which fund' matters far less than most people assume.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated Jan 12, 2026 8 min read

Quick answer: A mutual fund pools money from many investors, and a professional fund manager invests that pool in a basket of stocks or bonds according to a stated strategy. You own units, priced by NAV, and your money moves up or down with what the fund holds. The specific fund you pick matters less than getting the category right for your goal and staying invested long enough for it to work.

"Which mutual fund should I buy" is the question everyone asks first, and it's genuinely the wrong starting point. Before comparing fund options, it helps to understand what a mutual fund actually is — because once that's clear, most of the anxiety around "picking the right one" goes away.

Key takeaways

  • A mutual fund is a pool of investor money, professionally managed toward a stated objective.
  • NAV is simply the price per unit — a higher or lower NAV says nothing about fund quality.
  • Category (equity, debt, hybrid) matters more for your decision than the specific fund name.
  • Expense ratio and consistency of process matter more than one year's chart-topping return.

What a mutual fund actually is

Think of a mutual fund as a large shared basket. Thousands of investors each put money into that basket. A fund manager — a qualified professional, regulated by SEBI — decides what goes into the basket: which stocks, which bonds, in what proportion, based on the fund's stated strategy (e.g. "large-cap equity" or "short-duration debt").

You don't own the underlying stocks directly. You own units of the fund itself, and the value of your units rises and falls with the combined value of everything in the basket.

What NAV actually means

NAV — Net Asset Value — is the price of one unit of the fund, published once at the end of each trading day (unlike a stock price, which moves continuously). It's calculated as:

NAV = (Total value of fund's holdings − Expenses) ÷ Number of units outstanding

💡
A frequent misconception: people assume a fund with NAV ₹500 is "expensive" and one with NAV ₹15 is "cheap" or has "more room to grow." Neither is true. NAV level says nothing about future returns — it's simply a function of how long the fund has existed and how its assets have performed, not a discount or premium indicator.
Category and consistency matter more than picking the single "best" fund.

Why "which fund" matters less than you'd think

Within a well-chosen category — say, large-cap equity funds for a 10-year goal — most funds from reputable houses tend to deliver broadly similar long-term outcomes, before you account for expense ratio differences. The bigger decisions are:

  • Category fit: does the fund's risk level match your time horizon and goal?
  • Expense ratio: lower ongoing costs compound in your favour over decades.
  • Staying invested: switching funds chasing last year's topper usually costs more (in exit loads, taxes, and mistimed re-entry) than it gains.
