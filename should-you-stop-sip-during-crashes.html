Should You Stop SIPs During Market Crashes?

Markets go up, markets go down — here's what the data suggests about continuing your SIP through a downturn instead of pausing it.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated May 16, 2024 7 min read

Quick answer: Generally, no — continuing your SIP through a downturn means buying more units at lower prices, which tends to improve long-term returns once markets recover. Stopping usually means missing the recovery entirely, since the strongest recovery days often cluster close to the worst days.

Every market downturn brings the same question: should I pause my SIP until things stabilize? It's an understandable instinct — nobody enjoys watching a portfolio value drop. But the data on this is fairly consistent, and it points the opposite direction from instinct.

Key takeaways

  • Continuing SIPs during a downturn means buying more units at a lower average cost.
  • Markets often recover faster than expected — missing even a few of the best days materially hurts returns.
  • Timing an exit and a re-entry correctly is difficult even for professionals, let alone individual investors.
  • A SIP's entire design benefit — rupee-cost averaging — is strongest specifically during volatile periods.

Why continuing usually works in your favour

A SIP invests a fixed amount each month regardless of price — so when the market (and NAV) is down, that same amount buys more units than it would at a higher price. This is called rupee-cost averaging, and it's most effective specifically during the periods that feel worst to stay invested through.

Markets go up. Markets go down. You stay invested.

The cost of missing the recovery

Market recoveries are often sharp and concentrated in a small number of trading days that are impossible to predict in advance. Long-term studies of major indices consistently show that missing even the 10 best trading days over a decade — often clustered right after the worst days — can cut total returns substantially compared to staying invested throughout.

💡
This isn't a claim that markets always go up in the short term, or that downturns don't matter. It's specifically about the SIP mechanism: pausing removes the exact benefit — buying more units cheaply — that a downturn otherwise provides a long-term investor.

When pausing might make sense

The exception isn't about market conditions at all — it's about your own situation. If a downturn coincides with a genuine cash flow problem (job loss, emergency expense) and you don't have an emergency fund to cover it, pausing the SIP is reasonable. The mistake is pausing purely because the portfolio value looks lower on a screen.

What to do next

If you're currently tempted to pause a SIP, it's worth checking first whether that's driven by market conditions or an actual cash flow need — those call for different responses. Happy to think it through together on a free 30-minute call if you're unsure.
