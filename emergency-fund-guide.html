Emergency Fund: How Much Is Actually Enough?

Why this comes before investing anywhere else, how to size it for your actual situation, and where it should live.

TJ
Thejus Joseph Updated Jan 16, 2026 6 min read

Quick answer: Aim for 3–6 months of essential expenses if you're salaried with stable income, and 6–12 months if your income is variable or you're a sole earner. Keep it in a savings account, sweep-in FD, or liquid fund — not in equity, where it could lose value exactly when you need it.

An emergency fund is the least exciting part of personal finance, which is exactly why it gets skipped. But it's the single thing that determines whether an emergency becomes a temporary setback or a reason to sell investments at a loss.

Key takeaways

  • 3–6 months of expenses for stable, salaried income; 6–12 months for variable income or sole earners.
  • Size it against essential expenses only, not your full current lifestyle spend.
  • Keep it liquid and low-volatility — a savings account, sweep-in FD, or liquid fund, never equity.
  • Build it before investing elsewhere, not "alongside" or "after" — it's the foundation, not a parallel goal.

How to size your fund correctly

Start with your essential monthly expenses — rent, groceries, utilities, insurance premiums, minimum debt payments — not your total spending including discretionary items. Then multiply by the number of months appropriate to your situation:

  • 3 months: stable government or large-company job, no dependents, dual-income household
  • 6 months: typical salaried professional, standard baseline for most people
  • 9–12 months: freelance/variable income, business owner, or sole earner supporting dependents

Use the Emergency Fund Calculator to plug in your own numbers and get a specific target.

The fund's job is protection, not growth — those are two different goals.

Why equity is the wrong place for this money

Markets can fall 15–20% within weeks during a downturn. If your emergency fund is invested in equity and a job loss or medical emergency coincides with that dip, you're forced to redeem at a loss — turning one problem into two. The entire value of an emergency fund is that its amount is known and available regardless of what markets are doing that week.

💡
A useful split for larger emergency funds: keep 1–2 months in a regular savings account for instant access, and the remainder in a liquid mutual fund or sweep-in FD, which still allows withdrawal within a day but usually earns a bit more than a plain savings account.

What to do next

Once your fund is fully sized, it's the green light to start directing money toward actual investing goals — see Your First SIP: A Step-by-Step Guide for what comes next.
